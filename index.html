Animals losing their home

As widely researched by NASA, temperatures in the Arctic rapidly rise, having an impact on the melting of ice. Over the last 100 years, the temperature has risen almost twice as much as the global average rate. The earlier disintegration and melting of the Arctic sea ice in the spring and then later freezing in the winter leave the sea unfrozen for a long time. This phenomenon currently affects several species of arctic animals. Ice cover is changing rapidly, in both extent and thickness, and shrinking far too quickly for these species to adapt. We will focus on the most endangered species of animals, such as polar bears, foxes, seals, and walruses.

POLAR BEARS

Why are polar bears so important?

Polar bears help us gain an understanding of what is happening throughout the Arctic. They are large carnivores meaning they are at the top of the food chain - and are particularly important indicators of the health of an ecosystem.


How does climate change affect polar bears?

Big problem with hunting:
Polar bears rely on sea ice to hunt and store energy for the summer and autumn, when food can be scarce. Sea ice now melts earlier in the spring and forms later in the autumn in the bears’ southern range, like Hudson Bay and James Bay in Canada. As the bears spend longer periods without food, their health declines. For every week earlier the ice breaks up in Hudson Bay, bears come ashore roughly 10 kg lighter and in poorer condition.

POLAR FOXES

Why are polar foxes so important?

Organic waste from the foxes and their kills make the area surrounding their dens more fertile, leading to almost three times as many dune panes of grass, willows, and wildflowers to sprout up, compared to the rest of the tundra.


How does climate change affect polar foxes?

Big problem with hunting: Because Arctic foxes rely heavily on lemmings for food, climate change impacts on lemmings can have profound effects on foxes. During the winter, lemmings live in spaces under the snow that provide insulation from the cold, protection from predators, and access to plant foods. Rising temperatures due to climate change can cause poor snow conditions for lemming life. These poor snow conditions can cause the gradual extinction of polar foxes.

WALRUSES

Why are walruses so important?

Walruses can have a large effect on their prey and play an important role in the Arctic ecosystem by influencing the structure of benthic invertebrate communities. They can eat more than 50 clams during a single 7-minute dive to the seafloor and consume 35–50 kilograms.


How does climate change affect walruses?

Walruses depend on sea ice as platforms for feeding and to rest, and the warming Arctic disrupts their daily lives. In the past decade, the earlier melting of sea ice in the summer has caused the flushes to float to the shores of the ocean. SEALS

Why are seals so important?

Seals are top predators. They eat fish, squid, and crustaceans, including some of the species that fishermen catch. Seals also have the unique ability to contribute nutrients to the edge of the ocean they are contributors to restoring vegetation on nutrient-limited islands.


How does climate change affect seals?

According to the Union of Concerned Scientists, almost no seal pups, dependent on sea ice, survived during the ice-free years of 1967, 1981, 2000, 2001, and 2002. Environmental scientists, Dr. Clive McMahon and Dr. Harry Burton of the Australian Antarctic Division have concluded that the warming climate is changing the ocean’s ecology to such a degree that the survival of seals and their young has increasingly become a concern for marine biologists. Endangered marine wildlife of the Arctic Ocean

SSome fish species that can be found on the Arctic Ocean are on the list of the endangered species created by the IUCN; for example, the Chum salmon and the Atlantic salmon (that can be found on the Arctic Ocean despite its name) are considered as threatened by the environmental changes. They have been threatened by the changes in temperature of the water and the alterations in sea currents. More than being threatened, some of them are already considered as extinct in the wildlife by the IUCN like the Beloribitsa: it is considered extinct in the wild because it’s a fish that has a long way to migrate for its reproduction (more than 3,000km) and because of human construction and the changes on the sea, it is impossible for them to actually do it again. To explain how hard climate change is on the Arctic Ocean, there is the Dolly Varden Trout case. This fish can be found in a lot of different seas in the world but is listed as a species of special concern in the Arctic sea.

Sea currents are changing

The Beaufort Gyre is a powerful oceanic current that carries cold freshwater swirling around underneath the arctic icecap. Powered by polar winds, the current protects the ice sheet from warm water residing underneath. As the arctic ice sheet thins with a warming planet, the polar winds become more effective, making the current stronger. The Gyre thus picks-up more freshwater, which eventually gets released all at once when the direction of the current changes, every 5 to 7 years. The sudden release of this water all at once has the potential to induce catastrophic effects on western Europe. Polar Amplification & The Arctic

Polar amplification refers to the melting of the polar ice sheets exacerbating the effects of global warming. Ice reflects about 50 to 70 percent of solar rays back into space, as opposed to the ocean’s meager 6 to 7 percent. With roughly 14 million square kilometers surface level ice, the arctic is one of the most crucial elements in maintaining a stable climate on Earth. As it melts, the effects of solar rays become much more severe, leading to a dangerous cycle of climate change that will affect generations to come. Ocean acidification

Ocean Acidification refers to the increasing levels of pH in the world’s oceans, as a result of CO2 pollution. The delicate balance of the world’s ecosystems is heavily disrupted by this, causing mass deaths within the food chain. This has a ripple effect that eventually reaches us, as seen in, for instance, the Atlantic cod, which is a stable for economies of Northern Europe. Soc!al !mpact

The indigenous people of the Arctic have lived in their homeland for millennia. The societies have ingrained the land with meaning; they have adapted and evolved along with their understanding and respect of their world. The land and waters of the arctic anchors indigenous societies. The environment is therefore a big building block for the culture to provide resources and for them to continue to survive.

Problem to find food for inuit population

Changes in the ocean ecosystems, like warming water, or currently emphasized ocean acidification are a major threat for the coastal communities, who rely on arctic species, the distribution of which depends on the sea ice. In other words, the ice is a pathway for transport and hunters that can no longer trust their traditional knowledge and experience. Climate change has an influence on the food chain of marine wildlife and affects people engaged in fisheries.

They have to adapt themselves to a changing environment

The indigenous people of the Arctic are threatened by climate change in many aspects of their life. As we mentioned before, some of the threats are access to food, the integrity of local infrastructure and the ability to maintain their traditional lifestyle. Despite all the threats climate change is giving the indigenous people, they have learned to adapt on an individual basis with new hunting techniques that implements modern technology. They are paying attention to the fluctuations of wildlife populations and migration patterns and adjusting accordingly.

Weather changing = they have to face threats

The climate change is not only threatening the indigunous people by making their livelihood more difficult than it already is, it is also changing the weather which can cause more snowstorms and arctic blizzards. What can ! do?

With the drastic changes in the Arctic marine environment soon to cause irreversible consequences that will be felt worldwide, it is integral that we, as individuals, all do our part to minimize our climate impact. Bellow, we have 3 easy steps which you can take to contribute to the protection of the very delicate Arctic region.

Donate to the Ocean Conservancy


The Ocean Conservancy is a non-profit organization focused on conservation efforts in the ocean. Recently, they’ve worked with various partners to raise awareness of the many issues facing the Arctic ocean and working towards finding and implementing solutions to alleviate these problems. You can easily contribute to this organization through a form, where you enter your contact details and details about payment.

Participate in politics


One of the biggest contributors to the change in our climate are industries and a lack of conservation efforts by the world’s governments. Although you may feel small individually, by comparison, one of our most powerful tools is our right to vote. Participate in rallies, volunteer for your local environmental agencies, and biggest of all: vote! Stay informed on the policies of up-and-coming politicians, and let them know where you stand.

Reduce your personal impact


A few simple changes in your daily habits can go a long way in reducing the overall impact of humans on the climate. The New York Times has published a very informative article on the steps you can personally take to reduce your impact. Among these are: making your home more energy-efficient with the use of LED light bulbs and conservation of heat, buy less new and buy more second-hand and think about the ways you travel. CLICK
